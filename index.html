Supernovae and Human Evolution

Introduction

Supernovae bombarded Earth with cosmic energy starting as many as 8 million years ago, with a peak some 2.6 million years ago, initiating an avalanche of electrons in the lower atmosphere and setting off a chain of events that feasibly ended with bipedal hominins such as homo habilis, dubbed "handy man."

Key Points

Cosmic Energy and Earth

Supernovae bombarded Earth with cosmic energy starting as many as 8 million years ago, with a peak some 2.6 million years ago.

Effect on Atmosphere

The cosmic energy initiated an avalanche of electrons in the lower atmosphere, possibly increasing cloud-to-ground lightning strikes.

Impact on Evolution

The increased lightning strikes could have ignited forest fires, leading to savannas and prompting ancestors of homo sapiens to walk upright.

Conclusion

While supernovae could have influenced the evolution of bipedalism in human ancestors, no such events are likely to occur again soon. The nearest star capable of a supernova is Betelgeuse, which is too far to have a significant impact.
